\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

This section surveys recent research along four dimensions: traditional QoS routing, DRL-based routing, GNN-based network modeling, and conditioning mechanisms for graph networks.

\subsection{Traditional QoS Routing and Traffic Engineering}

Classical mechanisms such as OSPF, ECMP, and CSPF forward traffic hop by hop according to static link weights and lack differentiated service capability under mixed traffic. Wang and Crowcroft established the constraint-optimization framework for QoS routing~\cite{Wang1996_QoSRouting}, and Fortz and Thorup exposed the limitations of weight-optimization-based traffic engineering under dynamic demand~\cite{Fortz2000_TrafficEngineering}. Brundiers et al.\ studied the benefits of loops in Segment Routing for traffic engineering, demonstrating the potential of flexible path control for dynamic load balancing~\cite{Brundiers2021_Loops_SR_TE}, and further proposed a midpoint optimization approach for finer-grained traffic engineering control~\cite{Brundiers2022_Midpoint_SR_INFOCOM}. These methods share a common shortcoming, namely their myopia: each decision is optimized independently and cannot reserve resources for heterogeneous flows from a global or long-term sequential perspective. When mouse flows and elephant flows share links, static strategies often fail to simultaneously satisfy strict latency guarantees and high throughput.

\subsection{DRL-Based Network Routing}

To overcome the limitations of fixed rules, researchers have introduced DRL into routing decisions. Liu et al.\ proposed DRL-OR, which handles heterogeneous service demands with multi-objective DRL~\cite{Liu2021_DRLOr_INFOCOM}. Casas-Velasco et al.\ proposed DRSIR, achieving intelligent routing decisions in SDN via deep Q-learning~\cite{CasasVelasco2021_DRSIR_TNSM}. Chen et al.\ proposed an adaptive multipath routing optimization framework based on multi-agent meta-reinforcement learning~\cite{Chen2022_MultiagentMeta_TNNLS}. Suzuki et al.\ proposed a multi-agent DRL method that jointly optimizes computation offloading and routing in multi-cloud edge networks, validating the adaptive capability of multi-agent collaboration in dynamic environments~\cite{Suzuki2023_MADRL_Routing_TNSM}. Sanchez et al.\ proposed DQS, achieving routing optimization in SDN through QoS-driven DRL~\cite{Sanchez2024_DQS_JPDC}. These methods nonetheless share two limitations. First, their policy networks encode network state mostly through fully connected layers or simple embeddings, lacking explicit modeling of topological structure and thus suffering from limited generalization across diverse topologies. Second, they typically optimize a single global objective for all flows (e.g., minimizing maximum link utilization or average latency) and do not embed per-flow QoS intent as a conditioning signal in the policy network, so they cannot achieve differentiated routing under mixed traffic.

\subsection{GNN-Based Network Modeling and Routing}

GNNs provide a natural tool for encoding network topology, and existing research proceeds along two directions.

The first is offline performance modeling. Rusek et al.\ proposed RouteNet~\cite{Rusek2020_JSAC_RouteNet} and Ferriol-Galm\'{e}s et al.\ proposed RouteNet-Fermi~\cite{FerriolGalmes_2023_RouteNetFermi}, both using message-passing neural networks to predict link delay and packet loss. Huang et al.\ proposed xNet, which achieves flow-level QoS prediction by learning temporal state transitions~\cite{Huang2024_ToN_xNetModeling}. These methods target offline modeling under aggregated traffic, learning ``one graph, one global embedding,'' and cannot adapt to online per-flow decisions.

The second is graph-aware policies for online decision-making. Almasan et al.\ validated the routing-quality improvement obtained by jointly applying GNNs and DRL~\cite{Almasan2022_RLmeetsGNN}; Bern\'{a}rdez et al.\ proposed MAGNNETO, a multi-agent GNN system for distributed traffic engineering~\cite{Bernardez2023_MAGNNETO_TCCN}; Yang et al.\ and Dong et al.\ explored graph-aware joint optimization of routing and scheduling in time-sensitive networking and network slicing, respectively~\cite{Yang2022_GCN_RL_IoTJ,Dong2022_GCN_Slicing_Routing_TCCN}; and Bhuvanasi et al.\ achieved resilient routing using GNNs and multi-agent DRL~\cite{Bhuvanasi2023_ResilientRouting_TNSM}. Although these methods bring GNNs into online decision-making, they still produce a unified topological representation for all flows. When mouse flows and elephant flows arrive sequentially, the system sees the same embedding of the same graph and cannot perceive differences based on per-flow QoS characteristics, let alone make different routing decisions accordingly. Collectively, these works constitute the paradigm that couples GNN topology encoding with DRL path selection. The absence of flow conditioning makes this paradigm a natural but undifferentiated baseline.

\subsection{Conditioning Mechanisms for GNNs}

To let network representations adjust dynamically according to external conditions, researchers have proposed various conditioning mechanisms. These can be grouped by the level at which the conditioning signal operates into three categories:
(1)~\textbf{Node-level conditioning}, such as the GNN-FiLM of Brockschmidt et al.~\cite{Brockschmidt2020_GNNFiLM}, which generates affine modulation parameters from target node features to reshape the message-passing process directly; (2)~\textbf{Edge-level dynamic weights}, such as the GATv2 of Brody et al.~\cite{Brody2022_GATv2}, which computes adaptive attention weights through the dynamic interaction of node features, conditioning the strength of relations; and (3)~\textbf{Type-level conditioning}, such as the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer of Hu et al.~\cite{Hu2020_HGT}, which uses node type as a semantic condition to differentiate feature transformations across relation patterns.

In all of these mechanisms, however, the conditioning signal originates from graph-intrinsic structure (target nodes, edge features, node categories, and the like) rather than from per-flow external QoS intent. In the online routing scenario, what distinguishes embeddings is the QoS characteristics of the arriving flow: the same topology, when facing a mouse flow versus an elephant flow, should produce markedly different node representations and routing decisions. No prior work has brought FiLM conditioning into per-flow online routing, and differentiated topology perception and policy learning remain open problems.

In summary, the existing literature exhibits clear gaps: (1)~traditional methods are myopic and cannot handle mixed QoS; (2)~DRL methods lack explicit topological modeling; (3)~GNN methods assume homogeneous traffic and suffer from the ``same graph, same embedding'' representational homogeneity; and (4)~conditioning mechanisms for GNNs have not been applied to per-flow online routing. QoS-FiLM addresses these limitations.
