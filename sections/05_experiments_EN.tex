\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

 The experiments are conducted on three backbone topologies (Fig.~\ref{fig:topologies}). To train a policy that generalizes across network states, all link attributes are randomly regenerated at the start of each episode: bandwidth is sampled from $\text{Uniform}[30,100]$ and delay from $\text{Uniform}[1,20]$. Traffic generation treats the QoS intent $\phi$ as the dominant variable: $\phi$ is drawn from a flipped Pareto distribution ($\alpha\!=\!0.2$), so that the overwhelming majority of flows fall in the low-$\phi$ region (mouse flows with strict bandwidth and delay constraints) and only a small number are high-$\phi$ flows (elephant flows with relaxed constraints). The bandwidth demand $b$ is obtained from $\phi$ through a positively correlated linear mapping (Eq.~\eqref{eq:bw_gen}), with $b_{\text{max}}\!=\!40$ and a Gaussian noise standard deviation equal to $0.15$ times the span, so that $\phi$ and $b$ remain strongly positively correlated (Pearson $r\!\approx\!0.90$) yet not fully deterministic, with $\phi$ still carrying information independent of $b$.


 Each episode contains $W=150$ flows arriving sequentially, requiring the policy to maintain service quality under long-range credit assignment. Randomized topologies and flow generation ensure that the agent cannot memorize fixed paths and must instead make differentiated routing decisions based on the current link states and the intent of each flow. For ABILENE, a smaller $W=50$ is used due to its fewer nodes and lower total capacity.

We compare against five methods spanning two categories. Classical routing algorithms: OSPF, ECMP, and CSPF. Learned routing agents: (i)~noFiLM-Tf, a standard GNN-based DRL policy with TransformerConv message passing and no flow conditioning. This architecture is representative of the GNN-based DRL paradigm. (ii)~MLP, a non-graph MLP policy that tests whether graph structure awareness is necessary. noFiLM-Tf shares identical network depth, width, and training protocol with QoS-FiLM, ensuring that any performance gap is attributable to FiLM conditioning rather than architectural differences. All methods are evaluated on the same $200$ test traffic sequences (episodes); learned agents use deterministic actions (selecting the highest-probability candidate among the $K$ paths) to ensure reproducibility.

We report Mean Reward (Mean $R$), Bandwidth Satisfaction Score (BW Score, the mean of the per-flow bandwidth satisfaction ratios $\rho_t$), and Average End-to-End Delay (Avg Delay, the mean of the effective delays $\hat D_t$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:delay}) across all flows). Bandwidth scores are further broken down by flow category (small mouse / mid / big elephant) to examine whether the policy differentiates its treatment by QoS intent.

\subsection{Hyperparameters and Implementation Details}
\label{sec:exp:hyperparam}

All hyperparameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparam}. For the model architecture, the hidden dimension $h=256$ and the 4-layer GNN follow the common configuration ranges $h\in[64,256]$ and $L\in[2,5]$ adopted by RouteNet~\cite{Rusek2020_JSAC_RouteNet} and GNN-FiLM~\cite{Brockschmidt2020_GNNFiLM} in routing scenarios, and the single attention head matches the default setting of prior work~\cite{Shi2021_UniMP}. For PPO training, $\gamma=1.0$ is used because the task involves finite-horizon (episodic) episodes; the remaining clipping coefficient, GAE parameters, and value coefficient follow the defaults of the original PPO papers~\cite{Schulman2017_PPO,Schulman2016_GAE}, while the entropy coefficient $c_e=0.01$ and the minibatch size were determined by grid search. For the task, Pareto $\alpha=0.2$ pushes the proportion of low-$\phi$ flows above 80\%, matching the heavy-tailed traffic characteristics~\cite{CISCO2023_GlobalTraffic}. the ratio of the flow bandwidth upper bound $b_{\text{max}}=40$ to the link capacity lower bound $30$ allows a single flow to create a bottleneck, providing a learning signal for the policy to reserve capacity. The delay normalization constant $\tau=38.3$ is determined by the delay under ECMP routing. All variants share identical hyperparameters. The implementation is based on PyTorch, TorchRL, and PyTorch Geometric.

\subsection{Comparison with Classical and Learned Baselines}
\label{sec:exp:main}

%We first compare QoS-FiLM against classical routing algorithms to quantify the advantage of flow-aware representations. To isolate the ``learned policy vs.\ classical algorithm'' comparison, this section contrasts QoS-FiLM with the four classical baselines; ablation comparisons against the noFiLM-Tf and MLP (no message-passing) variants are deferred to Section~\ref{sec:exp:repr}.
This experiment assesses whether QoS-FiLM improves upon both classical heuristics and the learned baseline noFiLM-Tf, which shares our architectural backbone.

At $W\!=\!150$, QoS-FiLM outperforms all baselines on all three metrics of mean reward, bandwidth satisfaction, and delay (Fig.~\ref{fig:main_bars}): its mean reward of $+0.121$ exceeds that of the strongest classical baseline CSPF ($-0.046$) by $+0.167$, its BW Score of $0.759$ exceeds CSPF's ($0.473$) by $0.286$, and its average delay drops from $125.9$ to $112.9$~ms.Although CSPF attains the lowest delay among the classical baselines ($125.9$~ms), it does so at the expense of bandwidth satisfaction (BW Score only $0.473$), resulting in a mean reward ($-0.046$) far below QoS-FiLM's $+0.121$. This confirms that delay alone is an insufficient proxy for overall service quality; QoS-FiLM achieves both lower delay and superior reward by jointly optimizing bandwidth satisfaction and latency. This advantage holds across all three topologies: QoS-FiLM beats the respective strongest baseline CSPF on GEANT ($+0.121$ vs.\ $-0.046$), NOBEL-EU ($-0.108$ vs.\ $-0.183$), and ABILENE ($+0.122$ vs.\ $+0.041$), showing that \textcolor{blue}{flow-aware representations yield consistent gains without topology-specific hyperparameter tuning}. %In Fig.~\ref{fig:main_bars}, solid colored bars represent learned network variants (QoS-FiLM highlighted), while gray hatched bars represent static baselines.

QoS-FiLM also substantially outperforms the learned baseline noFiLM-Tf, which represents the GNN-based DRL paradigm without flow conditioning. On GEANT, QoS-FiLM attains a mean reward of $+0.121$ compared to noFiLM-Tf's $-0.098$ ($\Delta R\!=\!+0.219$), with higher bandwidth satisfaction ($0.759$ vs.\ $0.601$) and lower average delay ($112.9$ vs.\ $157.8$~ms). This advantage is consistent across topologies: on NOBEL-EU, QoS-FiLM reaches $-0.108$ versus noFiLM-Tf's $-0.282$; on ABILENE, $+0.122$ versus $+0.008$. Since noFiLM-Tf shares identical network depth, width, and training protocol with QoS-FiLM, this gap is attributable to FiLM conditioning rather than architectural differences. A detailed ablation decomposing this contribution is provided in \S\ref{sec:exp:repr}.

Source of Advantage: Non-Myopic Long-Range Resource Allocation. Classical algorithms, including the ``stepwise optimal'' CSPF, make independent greedy decisions for each flow and never reserve capacity for subsequent flows; under the long sequence $W\!=\!150$, early arrivals deplete critical link capacity and leave later flows with no viable path. Bucketing by $\phi$ confirms that QoS-FiLM disproportionately improves service for the most strictly constrained mouse flows, while all methods converge in the mid-intent region and score low in the high-intent region due to inherent resource scarcity (see Table~\ref{tab:film_w150_bucketed} for the detailed breakdown). This validates that routing is a sequential decision problem with long-range credit assignment, and CSPF is systematically surpassed by the learned non-myopic policy.

\subsection{Ablation on Message Passing and Intent Conditioning}
\label{sec:exp:repr}
The noFiLM-Tf baseline, introduced in \S\ref{sec:exp:main} as the representative GNN-based DRL method, simultaneously serves as the ablation counterpart to QoS-FiLM: the two are architecturally identical except that noFiLM-Tf removes FiLM conditioning and concatenates intent only at the final scorer. This controlled design isolates the contribution of FiLM from confounding factors such as model capacity or training variance.

To decompose this contribution further, we design three variants along the progressive path ``no graph structure $\to$ graph structure but no flow awareness $\to$ graph structure with flow awareness'' (Fig.~\ref{fig:main_bars}), all sharing the same TransformerConv backbone and hyperparameters and differing only in their representational mechanism:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{MLP} (no message passing): removes message passing, replacing $\mathrm{Conv}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:conv} with a per-node MLP (while retaining FiLM modulation) and ignoring the edge set $E$ and link features $f$, to test whether graph structure information is necessary.
\item \textbf{noFiLM-Tf} (message passing, but intent concatenated only at the end): removes the $\gamma,\beta$ modulation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:film} (equivalently $\gamma\equiv 0,\beta\equiv 0$); the intent vector $m_t$ does not enter message passing and is concatenated with path embeddings only at the final scorer, so all flows see the same graph.
\item \textbf{QoS-FiLM} (per-layer intent injection): our method, which modulates node representations at every layer through the FiLM affine transformation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:film}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Message passing is foundational.} The MLP baseline scores worst on all three metrics (mean reward $-0.177$, BW Score $0.520$), with all flows degenerating into the same candidate path (Table~\ref{tab:film_w150_bucketed} shows similar scores across categories, indicating undifferentiated routing that mirrors the single-path behavior of classical shortest-path heuristics; see OSPF in Fig.~\ref{fig:main_bars}). Without neighbor aggregation, the model cannot perceive the global distribution of link utilization and cannot differentiate routing across intents, confirming that graph structure information is indispensable for routing learning.

\textbf{Per-layer intent injection (FiLM) brings significant gains.} Under the same GNN backbone, per-layer intent-injected QoS-FiLM ($+0.121$) significantly outperforms end-only intent-concatenated noFiLM-Tf ($-0.098$), with $\Delta R\!=\!+0.219$; BW Score improves by $+0.158$ ($0.759$ vs.\ $0.601$), and average delay drops from $157.8$ to $112.9$ (a reduction of $44.9$~ms). Fig.~\ref{fig:repr_training} shows the Eval Reward training curves of the three variants. The disadvantage of noFiLM-Tf stems from concatenating intent information only at the end, so the message-passing layers produce the same topological embedding regardless of flow type. The final scorer, seeing only this static representation, cannot recover the per-flow differentiation that per-layer FiLM conditioning would have provided. This trend is consistent across all three topologies (Fig.~\ref{fig:repr_training}): QoS-FiLM clearly leads noFiLM-Tf and MLP, and converges faster with smaller variance (NOBEL-EU: QoS-FiLM $-0.108$ vs.\ noFiLM-Tf $-0.282$; ABILENE: $+0.122$ vs.\ $+0.008$).

In terms of final policy quality (Fig.~\ref{fig:conv_training}), QoS-FiLM (TransformerConv$+$FiLM) is optimal in mean reward ($+0.121$) and bandwidth score ($0.759$), leading FiLM-GATv2 ($-0.097$, $0.596$) by a substantial margin, with a BW Score gap of $+0.163$. A paired $t$-test across $600$ evaluation episodes ($200$ episodes $\times$ $3$ seeds, pairing the per-episode mean reward on identical traffic sequences) confirms that QoS-FiLM significantly outperforms FiLM-GATv2 ($t\!\approx\!45.0$, $p\!<\!0.001$), with QoS-FiLM achieving the higher mean reward in $96.2\%$ of test episodes. The performance difference between the two operators ultimately stems from their fundamentally different treatment of link attributes (delay, capacity, residual bandwidth $f_e=[d_e,c_e,b_e^{\text{res}}]$). %TransformerConv injects link attributes into both the attention key and the message value: the attention weight is $\alpha_{i,j}=\mathrm{softmax}((\mathbf{W}_3\mathbf{x}_i)^{\top}(\mathbf{W}_4\mathbf{x}_j+\mathbf{W}_6\mathbf{e}_{ij})/\sqrt{d})$ and the message is $\mathbf{W}_2\mathbf{x}_j+\mathbf{W}_6\mathbf{e}_{ij}$. The link feature $\mathbf{e}_{ij}$ modulates attention independently through a multiplicative interaction (inner product), so a high-residual-bandwidth link can receive higher attention weight without depending on target node features; at the same time, the link feature directly constitutes the message content, so aggregated information naturally carries link-quality signals. GATv2Conv~\cite{Brody2022_GATv2}, in contrast, mixes link and node features additively: $\alpha_{i,j}\propto\exp(\mathbf{a}^{\top}\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{\Theta}_s\mathbf{x}_i+\mathbf{\Theta}_t\mathbf{x}_j+\mathbf{\Theta}_e\mathbf{e}_{ij}))$. Link features are summed with the source and target node features before jointly entering the nonlinear activation, which strongly couples the link-quality signal with node features; the attention weight of a high-bandwidth link then depends heavily on the neighboring node representations, diluting the independent discriminative power of link attributes.
TransformerConv injects link attributes into both the attention key and the message value through a multiplicative interaction, so a high-residual-bandwidth link can receive higher attention weight independently of node features. GATv2Conv, in contrast, mixes link and node features additively before the nonlinear activation, coupling the link-quality signal with node 
representations and diluting its independent discriminative power. In a routing scenario, link residual bandwidth is a hard constraint signal for path selection and should influence neighbor aggregation independently and directly; TransformerConv's dual-channel injection of link attributes (key + value) naturally suits this need, whereas GATv2Conv's additive mixing weakens the independent expressiveness of link quality.

By $\phi$ bucket and bandwidth category (Table~\ref{tab:bucketed_combined}), QoS-FiLM again exhibits the ``low-down, high-up'' pattern described in Section~\ref{sec:exp:repr}: the delay in the low-intent bucket is only $77.6$ (GATv2 is $135.2$, a reduction of $57.6$~ms), while the delay in the high-intent bucket, $290.0$, is slightly higher than GATv2's $253.5$. TransformerConv identifies the $\phi$ semantics of flows more accurately than GATv2 and transfers delay resources from high-intent to low-intent flows. By bandwidth category (Table~\ref{tab:bucketed_combined}), QoS-FiLM's small/mid bandwidth scores ($0.932$/$0.857$) both lead FiLM-GATv2 ($0.667$/$0.618$) substantially, with slightly lower big-flow scores ($0.348$ vs.\ $0.456$), again attaining exceptionally high small/mid flow scores at a relatively small cost to big flows.

\subsubsection{Interaction Between Operator and FiLM}
\label{sec:exp:synergy}

We further examine whether operator choice and conditioning interact synergistically. Adding the noFiLM-GATv2 variant forms a $2\times2$ comparison of operator $\times$ conditioning (Table~\ref{tab:synergy_2x2}; the bottom row reports the FiLM gain $\Delta R$ on each operator).

The key finding is that \emph{the gain from FiLM strongly depends on the underlying operator}. On TransformerConv, introducing FiLM yields a large improvement of $\Delta R\!=\!+0.219$ ($-0.098\!\to\!+0.121$); on GATv2, the FiLM gain is only $\Delta R\!=\!+0.007$ ($-0.104\!\to\!-0.097$), negligible in practice. The two dimensions are thus not independently additive but exhibit a strong interaction: the aforementioned difference in how link attributes are handled amplifies the gap in FiLM effectiveness. TransformerConv injects link attributes independently into the attention keys and message values, providing a high-quality, link-level differentiable foundation for FiLM's per-layer intent modulation. GATv2Conv, by contrast, mixes link attributes into node features, so even when FiLM injects flow intent accurately at each layer, the intent signal struggles to translate into effective path differentiation through the diluted link-quality representations. The combination of TransformerConv and FiLM is therefore not a simple superposition of two independent advantages but a joint product in which neither component can be omitted.

\subsubsection{Graph Structure vs. FiLM Conditioning}
\label{sec:exp:decompose}
\textbf{Decomposing the contributions.} The $2\times2$ table isolates operator$\times$conditioning, but the MLP variant (FiLM retained, message passing removed) offers a complementary view: comparing MLP ($-0.177$, Table~\ref{tab:film_w150_bucketed}) against noFiLM-Tf ($-0.098$) approximately decomposes the contributions of graph structure and FiLM conditioning. The transition from MLP to noFiLM-Tf (adding message passing while removing FiLM) yields $\Delta R\!=\!+0.079$, capturing the pure benefit of graph structure; the further transition to QoS-FiLM (re-introducing FiLM on the TransformerConv backbone) yields $\Delta R\!=\!+0.219$. The two components play fundamentally different yet equally indispensable roles. Graph structure supplies the spatial priors of the topology (which links exist, how congestion propagates), without which FiLM has no meaningful canvas to act on; this is confirmed by MLP's poor performance ($-0.177$) despite retaining FiLM. FiLM conditioning, in turn, injects the per-flow intent semantics that tell the graph \emph{what to prioritize}, without which the GNN treats all flows identically; this is confirmed by noFiLM-Tf's limited performance ($-0.098$). Their combination reaches $+0.121$, far exceeding either component in isolation. The two are therefore not competing contributors but \emph{complementary necessities}: graph structure defines the search space, and FiLM governs how that space is navigated for each flow intent.

The parameter overhead of FiLM is an operator-independent constant of $+6{,}144$ (a relative overhead of only $+0.6\%\sim+2.0\%$, coming from the two intent$\to$hidden-dim linear layers $\mathrm{MLP}_\gamma,\mathrm{MLP}_\beta$). On the TransformerConv backbone, FiLM trades less than $1\%$ of parameters for an improvement of $\Delta R\!=\!+0.219$, an exceptionally high cost-effectiveness.



\subsection{Experiment IV: Interpretability Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:interp}

To further understand the mechanism of FiLM conditioning, this section examines how FiLM and noFiLM-Tf differ in responding to flow intent from two perspectives: the \emph{representation level} and the \emph{decision level}.



\textbf{Representation level: intent-driven hidden feature sensitivity.} Fixing the network topology and link utilization state, we inject flows with systematically varying QoS intent $\phi$ into the same (src, dst) node pair and observe the hidden features produced by the encoder. Over $8$ random network states and $\phi\!\in\!\{0.05,0.2,0.5,0.8,0.95\}$, we compute intent sensitivity (the total change in hidden representations caused by varying $\phi$ under the same state): the FiLM encoder reaches a sensitivity as high as $46.46$, whereas noFiLM-Tf reaches only $1.4\times10^{-6}$ (numerically zero), a difference of seven orders of magnitude (Table~\ref{tab:interp_repr}). Moreover, as $\phi$ increases from the baseline ($0.05\!\to\!0.95$), the drift of the FiLM representations increases monotonically ($0\!\to\!8.6\!\to\!25.8\!\to\!40.5\!\to\!46.5$), indicating that intent is smoothly and continuously encoded into node representations, while the drift of noFiLM-Tf stays at zero throughout. This confirms the ablation design at the architectural level (noFiLM indeed cannot perceive intent) and provides direct quantitative evidence that FiLM injects per-flow intent into node representations. Fig.~\ref{fig:embedding_separation} further shows the per-dimension hidden activations from both encoders: FiLM's activations differ markedly between the two flows across most dimensions, whereas noFiLM-Tf's activations completely overlap.

\textbf{Decision level: intent-driven path selection diversity.} Differences at the representation level ultimately manifest as changes in routing behavior. Over $200$ test episodes comprising $460$ (src, dst) pairs, we compute the entropy of the path selection distribution for both variants (in bits; higher entropy indicates more diverse selection and stronger differentiation by state and intent). FiLM's mean entropy is $2.235$, significantly higher than noFiLM-Tf's $1.962$ (paired $t\!\approx\!14.7$, $p\!<\!0.001$); on $76\%$ of (src, dst) pairs FiLM's entropy is higher, and FiLM uses $10.4$ distinct candidate paths on average versus only $7.9$ for noFiLM-Tf. The FiLM policy can therefore make differentiated path selections based on current link states and each flow's intent, whereas noFiLM-Tf, unable to perceive intent, relies on state alone and concentrates its selection on fewer candidate paths. This corroborates the sensitivity evidence from the representation level: \emph{the intent that FiLM injects into representations drives more diverse path selection}, linking representation-level differentiation to decision-level behavior.
