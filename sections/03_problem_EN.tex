\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

We formulate online per-flow routing as a stochastic optimization problem and show why it calls for a RL approach.

\subsection{Network and Traffic Model}
\label{sec:problem:model}

Consider a directed network $G=(V,E)$ with node set $V$ and link set $E$. Each link $e\in E$ has a delay $d_e$ and a bandwidth capacity $c_e$; let its residual bandwidth at a given time be $b_e^{\text{res}}\in[0,c_e]$, with initial value $b_e^{\text{res}}=c_e$.

Traffic arrives online in \emph{sequences} (episodes): each sequence contains $W$ flows that arrive one after another. The agent selects a route for every flow immediately upon arrival and cannot foresee subsequent flows. Once a flow is assigned a path, it occupies that path's bandwidth continuously for the duration of the sequence, following the ``permanent connection / infinite holding time'' assumption of online routing; we do not model flow departures. After a sequence ends, capacities are reset and a new batch of arrivals begins.

The $t$-th flow is described by a quadruple $\mathbf{f}_t = (o_t,\, v_t,\, \phi_t,\, b_t), t=1,\dots,W $, where $o_t, v_t\in V$ are the source and destination nodes, $b_t$ is the bandwidth demand, and $\phi_t\in[0,1]$ is the \emph{QoS intent coefficient}. The coefficient $\phi_t$ follows a flipped Pareto distribution (parameter $\alpha$), whose heavy tail concentrates in the low-$\phi$ region, so that the overwhelming majority of flows are mouse flows with strict latency and bandwidth constraints and only a small number are elephant flows with relaxed constraints. The bandwidth demand $b_t$ is obtained from $\phi_t$ through a positively correlated linear mapping with Gaussian perturbation:
\begin{equation}
b_t = b_{\text{floor}} + (b_{\text{max}}-b_{\text{floor}})\cdot\phi_t + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma),
\label{eq:bw_gen}
\end{equation}
so that $\phi_t$ and $b_t$ are strongly positively correlated (Pearson $r\approx 0.90$) yet not fully deterministic; $\phi_t$ thus still carries QoS information independent of $b_t$. Together, $(\phi_t, b_t)$ characterize a flow: an elephant flow is ``large-volume and tolerant of best-effort delivery,'' while a mouse flow is ``small-volume but demanding of strict guarantees.'' Flow sequences are sampled from a demand distribution $\mathcal{D}$, i.e., $(\mathbf{f}_1,\dots,\mathbf{f}_W)\sim\mathcal{D}$.

When processing each flow, the system precomputes $K$ candidate shortest paths $\mathcal{P}_t=\{p_t^{1},\dots,p_t^{K}\}$ ($K$-shortest paths by hop count). The routing decision assigns one path $p_t\in\mathcal{P}_t$ from this candidate set to carry $\mathbf{f}_t$. Once assigned, the residual bandwidth of the links along the path is reduced by the actual occupancy and is not released within the sequence, thereby changing the available capacity faced by later arrivals. This is the resource competition between earlier and later flows: capacity occupied by earlier flows shrinks the feasible set of later flows.

\subsection{Service Quality Metrics}
\label{sec:problem:metrics}

Given the path $p_t$ selected for $\mathbf{f}_t$, we define its service quality through the bandwidth satisfaction ratio and the effective end-to-end delay.

\textbf{Bandwidth satisfaction ratio.} The bandwidth a path can deliver is constrained by its bottleneck link. We define
\begin{equation}
\hat b_t = \min\!\Big(\min_{e\in p_t} b_e^{\text{res}},\; b_t\Big),
\qquad
\rho_t = \hat b_t/b_t,
\label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_t\in[0,1]$ is the bandwidth satisfaction ratio (since $\hat b_t\le b_t$ and $\hat b_t\ge 0$, the range is naturally bounded); $\rho_t=1$ indicates that the demand is fully satisfied.

\textbf{End-to-end effective delay.} We adopt a \emph{congestion-aware effective delay proxy} to capture how link occupancy affects delay: the fuller the link, the higher the effective delay. Let the residual capacity ratio after occupancy be $u_e = b_e^{\text{res}}/c_e\in[0,1]$, and define the path effective delay as
\begin{equation}
\hat D_t = \sum_{e\in p_t}\big(1-\log u_e\big)\, d_e,
\label{eq:delay}
\end{equation}
where the physical propagation delay $d_e$ is multiplied by a congestion penalty factor $(1-\log u_e)$ that grows with occupancy: when the link is idle ($u_e\!=\!1$) the factor equals $1$ and the effective delay reduces to the physical delay, whereas when the link is fully loaded ($u_e\!\to\!0$) the factor diverges. Eq.~\eqref{eq:delay} is not a physical delay derived from queueing theory but a log-barrier-style monotonic congestion cost that pushes the policy away from fully loaded links. In RL it serves only as a reward-shaping signal; its monotonicity (higher congestion $\Rightarrow$ higher cost), rather than its absolute value, drives policy learning. Compared with the classic M/M/1 queueing term $1/(1-\rho)$ and its hyperbolic divergence, this proxy grows more slowly and penalizes extreme congestion more mildly.

\subsection{Utility Function and Optimization Objective}
\label{sec:problem:objective}

The QoS intent $\phi_t$ determines what a flow ``cares about.'' We use $\phi_t$ to combine the bandwidth and delay metrics into a single per-flow utility:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
U(\mathbf{f}_t, p_t) &=
\underbrace{\big[(1-\phi_t)\,\mathbb{1}[\rho_t\!\ge\!1] + \phi_t\,\rho_t\big]}_{\text{bandwidth utility }U^{\text{bw}}_t} \\
&\quad +
\underbrace{(1-\phi_t)\,\frac{1}{1+\hat D_t/\tau}}_{\text{delay utility }U^{\text{dl}}_t},
\end{split}
\label{eq:utility}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a delay normalization constant (see Section~\ref{sec:exp:hyperparam} for the specific value).
Eq.~\eqref{eq:utility} imposes fundamentally different objectives on the two flow types:
\begin{itemize}
\item When $\phi_t\!\to\!0$ (mouse flow), the bandwidth utility reduces to a hard satisfaction indicator $\mathbb{1}[\rho_t\!\ge\!1]$ (scoring only when bandwidth is fully satisfied) and the delay utility weight reaches its maximum, i.e., the flow requires strict bandwidth guarantees and low latency.
\item When $\phi_t\!\to\!1$ (elephant flow), the bandwidth utility reduces to the continuous ratio $\rho_t$ (partial satisfaction scores proportionally) and the delay utility term is switched off, i.e., the flow pursues throughput maximization alone.
\end{itemize}


\textbf{Optimization objective.} The routing policy must maximize the cumulative utility over the entire sequence in expectation over the demand distribution $\mathcal{D}$:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\{p_t\}}\;
\mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{f}_1,\dots,\mathbf{f}_W)\sim\mathcal{D}}
\left[\sum_{t=1}^{W} U(\mathbf{f}_t, p_t)\right],
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where $\{p_t\}_{t=1}^{W}$ is the sequence of routing decisions, and each $p_t\in\mathcal{P}_t$ depends only on the current and historically observable information $\{\mathbf{f}_\tau, p_\tau\}_{\tau\le t}$ and the current network state under the online causal constraint; it cannot depend on future flows $\{\mathbf{f}_\tau\}_{\tau>t}$ that have not yet arrived.

In Eq.~\eqref{eq:objective}, given a flow and a path, both the utility $U(\mathbf{f}_t,p_t)$ and the update of residual bandwidth are deterministic and computable, which might suggest that the problem can be solved by classical optimization or greedy rules. The true difficulty, however, lies not in bookkeeping but in the \emph{unseen future}: flows arrive online one after another, and when selecting a route for the $t$-th flow the agent cannot foresee subsequent flows $\mathbf{f}_{t+1},\dots,\mathbf{f}_W$ (the online causal constraint). Were the entire flow sequence presented upfront, routing could certainly be cast as an integer program with a globally optimal solution; but such an offline optimum presupposes knowledge of the entire future, cannot be executed online, and can serve only as a post-hoc performance upper bound, not to mention that the problem itself is NP-hard. Because the future is unseen, each routing step alters the feasible set of subsequent flows through the residual bandwidth $b_e^{\text{res}}$: preferentially assigning mouse flows to low-latency, high-residual-bandwidth links may deplete the capacity later needed by elephant flows. Stepwise greedy approaches (e.g., CSPF, which selects the shortest constraint-satisfying path for each flow on arrival) are therefore necessarily myopic. In expectation over the demand distribution $\mathcal{D}$, the optimal policy must instead reserve capacity non-myopically and trade off across flows, i.e., attribute the cumulative utility realized at the end of the sequence back to earlier routing steps. This is precisely the structure that an MDP captures. Section~\ref{sec:method} accordingly formalizes Eq.~\eqref{eq:objective} as an MDP and presents the solution method.
